### Description

The economics of GLP-1 care are getting harder. CAC keeps rising, pricing keeps getting pushed down, and more patients are willing to switch provider for the cheapest pen.

That leaves providers with the worst possible equation: lower gross profit per month and a customer base full of switchers who may not stay long enough to pay back acquisition.

In this episode, I speak with Paul Sacher about where AI actually helps. The most interesting use case is identifying patients at risk of churning before they cancel: the patient anxious about side effects, stuck on a plateau, bored of the app, or unsure what happens once they reach their goal weight.

The payoff is commercial. If AI helps a provider retain even a small share of those patients, it improves LTV, CAC payback, and lifetime gross profit. If it does not, the business is still left with the same problem: expensive acquisition, lower margins, and switchers.

### LinkedIn - Investor Angle

The GLP-1 chatbot is not the story.

The story is whether AI can stop patients from churning before CAC payback ever happens.

I spoke to Paul Sacher about AI in GLP-1 care. The useful framing is not "can the AI answer a patient question?" It is whether the system can identify who is about to disengage, understand why, and intervene safely before the patient leaves.

That matters because the market is moving from access to retention.

For DTC platforms, better support should mean better LTV and better CAC payback.

For pharma, it means better persistence on therapy and better real-world outcomes.

For insurers, it means fewer failed starts, fewer treatment cycles, and a clearer view of total cost of care.

The first AI product in GLP-1 care may not be a chatbot.

It may be churn infrastructure.

### LinkedIn - Founder/Operator Angle

If your GLP-1 platform treats a new patient, a switcher, a plateau patient, and a maintenance patient the same, you are probably leaking LTV.

That was one of the big ideas from my conversation with Paul Sacher.

AI in obesity care is most interesting when it stops being a generic chatbot and starts becoming a system that understands where a patient is in the journey.

The patient in week one needs expectation setting and side-effect support.

The patient at month six may need plateau support, dose-transition guidance, or a maintenance plan.

The switcher may not want another beginner onboarding flow.

The maintenance patient may reorder less often, but may also be harder to reacquire elsewhere if the support layer is genuinely valuable.

The product question is not "how do we get people to use the app more?"

It is: which intervention works for which patient, at which moment?

### LinkedIn - Safety And Regulation Angle

Calling an AI product "wellness" does not make it wellness.

That was one of the clearest points from Paul Sacher in our conversation about AI and GLP-1 care.

If an AI system is helping a patient think through side effects, titration, tapering, or triage, the regulatory question is not what the marketing page says. It is what the system is actually doing.

This is where a lot of consumer health companies may be underestimating the risk.

Patients want fast, personalised support.

Clinical teams want leverage.

Operators want lower support costs.

But if the AI crosses into clinical decision support without the right evaluation, monitoring, escalation, and post-market surveillance, the business risk becomes very real.

The best AI companies in obesity care will not just build better demos.

They will build safer systems.
